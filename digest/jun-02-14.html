The Fortnight in One Paragraph

Back with a fortnight edition — two weeks of policy intelligence, deeper analysis, and more stories than usual. And what a fortnight it was. It started with monetary policy signals and fuel price anxiety, and ended with Indian sailors dying in a US military strike. The RBI held the repo rate at 5.25% but raised its inflation forecast and cut GDP growth projections — a sign that the West Asia crisis is no longer an external risk but a domestic economic reality. Three Indian seafarers were killed when the US struck a tanker enforcing the Hormuz blockade, triggering a sharp diplomatic confrontation between External Affairs Minister Jaishankar and US Secretary of State Rubio — a confrontation that will define the Modi-Trump meeting at the G7 on June 17. Meanwhile, the TMC's internal rebellion deepened into what may be the party's worst crisis, LPG prices were hiked for the third time in three months even as the government slashed Ujjwala subsidies, the Cockroach Janta Party brought a new generation of protesters to Jantar Mantar, Annamalai walked out of the BJP to launch a new Tamil Nadu movement, DK Shivakumar took oath as Karnataka's Chief Minister, and the Zojila tunnel breakthrough brought all-weather Kashmir-Ladakh connectivity closer to reality.

Deep Dive

When Allies Strike — The Hormuz Crisis Claims Indian Lives

What Happened

What began as a geopolitical story about US-Iran tensions in April became an Indian story in June. The US-imposed blockade of the Strait of Hormuz — now in its fourth month — has trapped over 20,000 Indian seafarers in a combat zone since the blockade was announced on April 12. India had already flagged attacks on commercial vessels as "unacceptable" in May. But this fortnight, the crisis escalated sharply.

On June 8, a US missile struck a tanker off the Oman coast carrying 24 Indian sailors, all of whom were rescued. By June 11, the US had struck a third ship with Indian crew in a single week. Three Indian sailors were killed in the strike on the Settebello tanker. India's External Affairs Minister S. Jaishankar protested the deaths in a call with US Secretary of State Marco Rubio, calling the attacks "deeply worrisome" and demanding they "must end immediately." Rubio's response was blunt: ships violating the US blockade "won't be tolerated."

Domestically, the opposition seized on the crisis. Rahul Gandhi accused PM Modi of acting "like an obedient servant" to the US after Indian sailors were killed — a line that dominated media coverage for days. The confrontation set the stage for what will be the most consequential Modi-Trump bilateral in over a year, on the sidelines of the G7 in France.

Why It Matters

For Indian Seafarers

Over 20,000 Indian seafarers remain stranded in or near the Strait of Hormuz, facing physical danger and severe psychological stress. The BBC reported that sailors are "stressed and exhausted" after months trapped. With the US now actively striking ships carrying Indian crew, the danger is no longer abstract — it is lethal.

For Energy Markets and the Indian Economy

Brent crude has risen above $97 per barrel. The Hormuz blockade has shaved 20% off global LNG supplies. The RBI explicitly cited the West Asia crisis as a risk to its growth and inflation forecasts this fortnight — the conflict is already baked into India's macroeconomic projections for FY27.

For India-US Relations

This is the most serious friction point in India-US ties since the diplomatic fallout over Operation Sindoor. The Jaishankar-Rubio exchange was not diplomatic pleasantry — it was a public confrontation between close partners. The USTR simultaneously signalled that a trade deal is not imminent, despite Trump's claims. The Modi-Trump bilateral at G7 now carries the weight of both sailor deaths and stalled trade talks.

For Domestic Politics

The crisis has opened a rare bipartisan pressure point. Rahul Gandhi's "obedient servant" attack and AIMIM's Owaisi raising the PM's silence on Indian deaths abroad have put the government on the defensive on a national security issue — a domain where the BJP typically controls the narrative.

What's Next

Modi and Trump meet at the G7 on June 17. India is pressing for safe passage guarantees for commercial vessels with Indian crew. Coverage since April shows no de-escalation pattern: each month has brought more attacks, more stranded crew, and higher oil prices. Trump has repeatedly hinted at a peace deal with Iran — on June 14 he claimed one would be signed "today" — but Iran has called for caution. Until the blockade lifts or India secures specific crew-safety guarantees, the risk to Indian seafarers and energy supply chains remains acute.

Notably absent from the coverage: no official Indian government press release or PIB statement has appeared on this topic, despite its severity. The diplomatic response has been conducted through calls and statements to media — not through formal government communications.

The US-imposed Hormuz blockade has gone from disrupting India's energy supply chain to killing Indian citizens — and how New Delhi navigates the Modi-Trump meeting this week will set the trajectory for the India-US relationship through 2026.

RBI Holds the Line at 5.25%, But the Warning Signs Are Flashing

The Reserve Bank held the repo rate at 5.25% for the third consecutive meeting, but the accompanying signals were stark: inflation forecast raised to 5.1% for FY27, GDP growth cut to 6.6% from 6.9%, and explicit warnings about oil prices, monsoon risks, and geopolitical uncertainty. Governor Sanjay Malhotra called the inflation target "sacrosanct" — not a reassurance, but a signal that rate hikes may be on the horizon. The RBI also announced five measures to attract foreign exchange inflows, including a special ECB window that could bring up to $20 billion — an acknowledgment that the rupee needs structural support, not just intervention.

TMC Unravels: West Bengal's Ruling Party Faces Its Worst Crisis

What started as internal grumbling has become a full-blown rebellion. TMC rebels claimed majority MLA support early in the fortnight; by mid-week, Rajya Sabha MP Sukhendu Sekhar Ray had resigned from both his seat and the party. Twenty TMC MPs wrote to the Speaker backing the NDA, triggering a formal split in the party's Lok Sabha unit. The CID searched Mamata Banerjee's Kalighat party office, Abhishek Banerjee wrote to the Speaker against the rebel faction, and by June 14, Mamata reshuffled the party's youth and women wings — removing Saayoni Ghosh and Mala Roy from key positions. The rebellion draws parallels to the Shiv Sena split in Maharashtra, with one crucial difference: TMC rebels appear to be getting institutional accommodation rather than resistance.

LPG Prices Hiked Again — and Ujjwala Subsidy Quietly Slashed

Domestic LPG prices were raised by ₹29 per cylinder on June 7 — the second hike in three months, following a ₹60 increase in March — as the Hormuz blockade continued to drive up global energy costs. A 14.2-kg cylinder in Delhi now costs ₹942. But the larger story came days later: the government quietly reduced the annual Ujjwala subsidy quota from nine cylinders to four. For the 10+ crore beneficiaries of the Pradhan Mantri Ujjwala Yojana — India's poorest households — this means five fewer subsidised cylinders per year, at a time when each refill costs more. The third round of fuel cost escalation since March, now compounded by a direct cut to the safety net.

4

🪳 The Cockroaches Take to the Streets

The Cockroach Janta Party — born online, built on exam-fraud anger — held its first physical protest at Jantar Mantar on June 6, drawing 1,200 people demanding Education Minister Dharmendra Pradhan's resignation. Founder Abhijeet Dipke flew in from the US, held his first press conference, and declared the movement would not stop until Pradhan resigns. By June 12, CJP had announced nationwide protests in Lucknow, Pune, and Hyderabad. The Guardian called it "one of the most unexpected challenges to the country's rightwing government."

What's striking isn't the protest — it's the response to it. Delhi Police granted permission. CJP publicly thanked police for "all possible cooperation." No FIR was filed. The Delhi High Court refused an urgent hearing on a plea against the protest. Dipke openly announced his return from the US, held press conferences, and organised a nationwide campaign — all without institutional friction.

The record tells a different story when other groups mobilise. University students protesting hostel conditions at NLSIU faced administrative stonewalling. Students in Jammu & Kashmir protesting harassment saw vandalism charges and arrests. In Pune, two students were booked by police over a dance performance at a government hostel. A JJP leader was booked and six arrested over a university protest in Hisar. The Supreme Court itself recently clarified sedition law precisely because of its routine deployment against protesters.

CJP's frictionless debut raises a question the movement itself hasn't asked: why did the same institutional machinery that routinely meets protest with FIRs, lathis, and shutdowns offer the cockroaches a clear runway?

The answer likely lies in who is protesting and what they are protesting. Exam fraud is a safe target that embarrasses a minister, not the state itself. But the asymmetry is worth noting, because it reveals what institutional tolerance in India actually depends on.

The completion is both an engineering milestone and a strategic one, given Ladakh's significance in India's border security calculus.

India Launches Producer Price Index in Statistical Overhaul

India announced it will introduce a Producer Price Index for the first time, set to gradually phase out the Wholesale Price Index over five years. The PPI — covering output, input, and services prices — aims to align India's inflation measurement with global standards. A structural reform in economic measurement that will change how inflation is tracked, debated, and acted upon for years to come.

Fortnight Trends: Week 1 vs Week 2

Week 1 (June 2-8) was dominated by institutional policy: the RBI's June MPC decision anchored the narrative, with LPG price hikes, the Karnataka CM transition, CJP's first protest, and the Malviya Nagar fire rounding out a policy-and-politics week. Week 2 (June 9-14) was dominated by crisis: the killing of Indian sailors by US forces escalated from incident to diplomatic confrontation, the TMC rebellion deepened with formal splits and CID searches, and the Ujjwala subsidy cut landed.

Categories shifted accordingly — Finance and Energy led Week 1, while Defence, Foreign, and Politics surged in Week 2. The June 14 spike alerts tell the story: Politics surged 30x, Technology 16.5x, and Governance 16.1x above baseline. The one story that carried across both weeks without interruption was the RBI thread, appearing in coverage on all 13 days. The most notable escalation from Week 1 to Week 2: the India-US relationship went from Trump praising "good friend" Modi to Rubio telling India that blockade violations "won't be tolerated."

Sector Spotlight: Energy

Energy was the fortnight's most consequential sector, driven by three converging forces: the Hormuz blockade's continued disruption of global oil supply (Brent above $97), domestic fuel price adjustments (LPG hiked ₹29, commercial LPG up ₹42, ATF cut 27% for international airlines), and the RBI's explicit flagging of energy costs as an inflation risk. Coverage was notably source-diverse — government sources covered the Ujjwala and ATF changes, while media focused on human and economic impact.

The sector has seen steadily intensifying coverage every month since the Hormuz blockade began in April, and the trajectory shows no signs of cooling as long as the blockade persists. The ₹10,000-crore ATF Price Stabilisation Fund approved by the Cabinet signals the government is treating energy cost volatility as a structural problem, not a transient one.

On Our Radar

Iran peace deal timeline. Trump claimed on June 14 that a deal would be signed "today" — Iran called for caution. If a deal materialises before the G7, it changes the Modi-Trump dynamic entirely. If it doesn't, the sailor deaths dominate.

RBI rate hike cycle beginning? Analysts at Standard Chartered and HSBC both read the June MPC as a precursor to hikes. The next MPC meeting in August will be the test — if inflation continues above 5%, the rate cycle turns.

CJP nationwide expansion. The movement announced protests in Lucknow, Pune, and Hyderabad, with Dipke warning of a return to Delhi on June 20. Whether the movement sustains momentum beyond the initial social media wave will determine if it's a moment or a movement.

TMC rebellion endgame. With 20 MPs backing the NDA and the Speaker's decision pending, the question is whether the TMC splits formally or Mamata manages a reconciliation. The Maharashtra Shiv Sena precedent — institutional recognition of the rebel faction — looms.
